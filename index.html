literature Review

Impact of Divorce on Adolescent Mental Health

Adolescents from divorced families face a range of psychological challenges that stem from the instability and emotional turmoil associated with parental separation. Several studies report that divorce often triggers symptoms of anxiety, depression, emotional withdrawal, and in some cases, suicidal ideation among adolescents (Haker, 2005; Ramadhani & Krisnani, 2019). The grieving process that accompanies divorce varies by individual, but the sense of loss, insecurity, and disruption of daily routines are commonly cited factors affecting emotional well-being.

In many cases, adolescents internalize the conflict and blame themselves for the parental separation, particularly if they have witnessed arguments involving their name or care (Haker, 2005). This internalization can result in persistent feelings of guilt and low self-worth, which in turn contribute to emotional instability and social withdrawal. Moreover, adolescents who experience intense inter-parental conflict are more likely to develop aggressive or depressive tendencies than those from families with low-conflict divorces (Pálmarsdóttir, 2015).

The emotional toll is often exacerbated by reduced parental involvement or inconsistent parenting following divorce. Adolescents may feel neglected or emotionally unsupported, especially in single-parent households where the caregiver also struggles with economic or emotional stress (Wolf, 2016). The disruption of daily routines, changes in residence or school, and the absence of one parent can deepen a teenager’s sense of disconnection.

The Influence of Divorce on Adolescent Self-Identity Development

Adolescence is a critical developmental phase marked by the quest for identity, autonomy, and personal values. During this stage, adolescents strive to answer questions such as “Who am I?” and “Where do I belong?” However, for adolescents from divorced families, the identity formation process is often disrupted by emotional instability, inconsistent parenting, and the absence of a stable family structure (Wahyuningsih, 2020).

Several studies suggest that the dissolution of parental roles can lead to confusion in understanding gender identity and social expectations. Adolescents who grow up without the consistent presence of either a mother or father figure may experience difficulty internalizing role models, which affects their perception of themselves and others (Andini, 2020). This disruption may hinder their ability to establish a coherent self-concept, especially in environments where communication within the family is minimal or conflictual.

Peer Pressure in Adolescents from Divorced Families

Peer pressure plays a pivotal role in adolescent development, as social belonging and acceptance become central during this life stage. For adolescents from divorced families, the emotional vacuum created by disrupted family dynamics often amplifies the need for peer validation.

In the absence of emotional support at home, peers often fill the role of confidants, role models, and emotional anchors. This dynamic can be constructive when peers promote healthy behaviors and positive coping, but it becomes problematic when peer groups engage in delinquent or high-risk activities. Research by Rachman (2022) shows that adolescents from broken homes are more likely to engage in behaviors such as truancy, smoking, substance use, and even petty crime when influenced by deviant peer groups.

The tendency to seek belonging and escape feelings of rejection or worthlessness drives many adolescents to submit to negative peer influences. Jumita (2023), in a case study, found that remaja yang mengalami tekanan sebaya sering merasa takut dikucilkan jika tidak mengikuti arus teman, termasuk dalam tindakan berisiko seperti bolos sekolah atau pergaulan bebas. This reflects how peer groups can become both a social support system and a risk factor, depending on the context and values they carry.

Protective Factors and Coping Strategies

Despite the psychological and social risks associated with parental divorce, numerous studies have emphasized the presence of protective factors that can buffer adolescents from its negative impact. Emotional support from at least one involved and nurturing parent remains one of the most significant predictors of resilience among adolescents (Wolf, 2016). The consistent presence of a caregiver who communicates openly, validates emotions, and maintains routines provides a sense of security during familial transitions.

In addition to parental support, schools and community environments play a vital role in helping adolescents cope with the aftermath of divorce. Guidance counselors, peer support groups, and structured extracurricular activities offer adolescents constructive outlets for emotional expression and social connection. According to Zaman et al. (2021), interventions that promote emotional literacy, self-regulation, and peer empathy contribute positively to mental health outcomes in adolescents from divorced families.

Some adolescents also exhibit strong internal coping mechanisms, such as self-reflection, goal-setting, and spirituality. Jumita (2023), in her case study, highlighted how adolescents who actively engaged in positive self-talk, prayer, or journaling were better able to regulate emotional responses and avoid maladaptive behaviors.

Empowering Adolescents Against Risky Behaviors through Maqasid al-Shariah Framework

Adolescence is a vulnerable phase where individuals seek identity, belonging, and independence. However, in the absence of strong moral foundations, family support, and spiritual guidance, many adolescents fall into risky behaviors under the influence of peer pressure.

Even more concerning, in Singkawang (2025), a teenage girl gave birth in secrecy and abandoned her newborn in a public restroom (SuaraKalbar.co.id, 2025). The case reflects the critical absence of communication, value education, and safe support systems in the adolescent’s life.
