Literature Review

Navigating Identity and Peer Pressure: A Literature Review on Adolescents from Divorce Families

üìò Introduction

Parental divorce profoundly impacts adolescent development, particularly in areas like emotional regulation, identity formation, and peer interaction. Adolescents at this stage are vulnerable due to their search for identity and belonging, which can be disrupted by family instability (Patusniari, 2023). Emotional insecurity and reduced parental presence often intensify reliance on peers, making adolescents more susceptible to peer influence (Fitriyani, 2018).

This review synthesizes recent literature to explore how peer pressure and identity development are shaped in adolescents from divorced families.

üß† Impact on Mental Health

Adolescents from divorced families frequently experience depression, anxiety, and self-blame (Haker, 2005; Ramadhani & Krisnani, 2019). These emotional responses are worsened by inconsistent parenting and disrupted daily routines. Protective factors such as emotional support and mental health services play a critical role in mitigating these effects (Zaman et al., 2021).

üßç‚Äç‚ôÄÔ∏è Self-Identity Development

Divorce disrupts adolescents‚Äô ability to form stable self-identities, often leading to confusion about gender roles and personal values (Andini, 2020; Wahyuningsih, 2020). Some adolescents may be forced into ‚Äúparentification,‚Äù adopting adult responsibilities prematurely, which hinders exploration and identity formation (Patusniari, 2023).

üë• Peer Pressure and Influence

Peer pressure becomes a double-edged sword for adolescents from divorced homes. While positive peers can provide emotional stability, negative groups may encourage high-risk behaviors such as substance use or truancy (Rachman, 2022). Jumita (2023) noted that fear of rejection drives adolescents to conform, even at the cost of their safety.

üõ°Ô∏è Protective Factors & Coping

Resilience is supported by nurturing parents, mentorship, spiritual reflection, and structured school environments. Adolescents engaging in prayer, journaling, or self-talk show greater emotional regulation (Wolf, 2016; Jumita, 2023). Community-based interventions and emotional literacy programs are also effective buffers (Zaman et al., 2021).

‚úÖ Conclusion

Parental divorce, while challenging, does not uniformly lead to negative outcomes. Peer dynamics and identity confusion are major concerns, but these can be addressed through holistic support systems. Further research should explore culturally sensitive coping strategies to support adolescents more effectively.

Integration of Maqasid al-Shariah

Empowering Adolescents Against Risky Behaviors through Maqasid al-Shariah Framework

üß† Introduction / Background of the Issue

Adolescence is a vulnerable phase where individuals seek identity, belonging, and independence. Without strong moral foundations, family support, and spiritual guidance, many adolescents fall into risky behaviors due to peer pressure.

Recent cases in Indonesia illustrate this urgency. In Indragiri Hulu, a student died due to bullying (MetroTV, 2024). National reports show a rise in physical and online bullying (Inilah.com, 2024). In Singkawang, a teenage girl abandoned her newborn (SuaraKalbar.co.id, 2025). These incidents reflect a lack of emotional literacy, safe support systems, and value education.

From the lens of Maqasid al-Shariah, these issues violate key protections: Hifz al-Nafs (life), Hifz al-Aql (intellect), Hifz al-Din (faith), and Hifz al-Nasl (lineage).

üéØ Objectives of the Project
  • Promote awareness of risky behavior consequences.
  • Strengthen resilience through value-based Islamic education.
  • Develop community-based supports rooted in Maqasid principles.
  • Ensure holistic development aligned with all five Maqasid objectives.
‚öñÔ∏è Integration of Maqasid al-Shariah
Maqasid Principle Application in the Project
Hifz al-Nafs (Protection of Life) Prevents self-harm, violence, and behaviors threatening safety.
Hifz al-Aql (Protection of Intellect) Encourages critical thinking and emotional awareness.
Hifz al-Din (Protection of Faith) Promotes self-worth through spiritual guidance and discipline.
Hifz al-Nasl (Protection of Lineage) Emphasizes responsibility in relationships and sexual behavior.
Hifz al-Mal (Protection of Wealth) Addresses delinquency and substance abuse linked to financial harm.
üõ†Ô∏è Proposed Plan: S.A.F.E. Teens Initiative (Self-Awareness and Faith Empowerment)
  • Peer Mentoring Circles: Youth-led safe spaces with mentor guidance on identity and choices.
  • Faith-Based Workshops: Activities on resisting peer pressure and applying Islamic values.
  • Media Campaign: Social media messages (TikTok/IG) about spiritual resilience.
  • Family Engagement: Parent education on communication and early intervention.
  • Spiritual Reflection: Halaqah-style journaling and group reflection activities.
üìà Expected Outcomes
  • Greater awareness of peer pressure and risky behavior risks.
  • Strengthened internal values and Islamic emotional literacy.
  • Improved parent-teen trust and dialogue.
  • Decrease in harmful youth behaviors.
  • Established Maqasid-based school/community support models.
‚úÖ Conclusion

The S.A.F.E. Teens Initiative presents a Maqasid-rooted solution to adolescent risk. With emphasis on Hifz al-Nafs, Aql, Din, and Nasl, this model empowers youth spiritually, emotionally, and socially ‚Äî creating a more resilient, morally grounded generation.
