WEEK 12: Disrupted Development: Young Parents, Delinquency, Role Reversal, Special Needs

    Role Reversal and Parentification

    Role reversal, or parentification, refers to a situation where a child or adolescent takes on responsibilities typically associated with adults, particularly parents. This reversal in family dynamics disrupts the natural developmental process and often emerges in families with absent, emotionally unavailable, or dysfunctional caregivers (Hooper et al., 2011).     Psychological Insights

    From a psychological standpoint, role reversal often disrupts key developmental milestones in adolescence. According to Erikson’s psychosocial theory, adolescents must navigate the stage of identity vs. role confusion. When they are prematurely burdened with adult-like roles such as caring for siblings, managing household duties, or emotionally supporting their parents—they may experience confusion, burnout, or identity suppression.     Case Illustration: “Mira’s Story”

    Mira (pseudonym), a 15-year-old adolescent, assumed the role of caregiver for her two younger siblings after her parents divorced. Her mother worked long hours, and Mira was expected to cook, clean, help with homework, and provide emotional support to her siblings. Although she performed these duties diligently, she expressed constant fatigue and a loss of interest in her studies and friendships.

    In therapy, Mira revealed a deep sense of guilt whenever she took time for herself. She feared that neglecting her duties would make her “selfish.” Her identity was largely shaped by being the “second mother.” This reflects a classic case of emotional and instrumental parentification, where the adolescent’s development is constrained by the need to fulfill adult responsibilities (Chase, 1999).

    Islamic Perspective: Justice and Protection of the Vulnerable

    From the lens of Maqasid al-Shariah, the concept of preserving the intellect (hifz al-‘aql) and lineage/family (hifz al-nasl) becomes highly relevant. Islam upholds the rights of children to be nurtured, educated, and emotionally protected—not burdened prematurely. Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) emphasized compassion toward children and the importance of age-appropriate roles.     Reflections and Implications

    I resonate with this topic because I’ve seen it firsthand in peers who were forced to grow up too soon, and in the emotional toll it took on them. I now recognize that supporting adolescents means recognizing invisible burdens they carry. Interventions should include:

    • Psychoeducation for caregivers
    • School-based mental health support
    • Culturally sensitive counselling that incorporates Islamic values and emotional literacy

    It also highlights the importance of empowering adolescents to differentiate between helping and self-sacrifice, and encouraging them to set healthy boundaries.
