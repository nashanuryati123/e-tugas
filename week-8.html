WEEK 8: New Media and Impact on Family and Peers’ Relationship with the Adolescent

    The Digital Transformation of Adolescent Development

    This week, I've been exploring how digital technology has reshaped the way adolescents form their identity, build relationships, and interact with their social environment. New media such as social media, instant messaging apps, and gaming platforms play a crucial role in the dynamics of family and peer relationships. This discussion is highly relevant to my interest in understanding adolescent risky behavior and the importance of a holistic developmental psychology approach.

    Identity and Social Comparison in the Digital World

    According to Erikson (1968), adolescence is the phase of identity vs. role confusion, where individuals strive to explore and form their sense of self. Social media offers vast opportunities for exploration but can also lead to identity confusion if social expectations gained through "likes," comments, or cyberbullying conflict with one's self-worth. As Davis et al. (2023) highlight, prosocial online behavior can help adolescents form a positive identity, yet social pressure from media can also exacerbate identity instability and anxiety. I've realized that adolescents may adopt a "false" identity by presenting themselves in ways that don't align with reality to gain social acceptance. This can impact their self-confidence and emotional stability.

    Social Learning & Social Comparison Theory

    Based on Bandura (1977), adolescents learn by observing others including public figures on social media. They internalize values and behaviors through modeling, both positive and negative. Coupled with Social Comparison Theory (Festinger, 1954), adolescents tend to constantly compare themselves to others. Upward comparison can trigger feelings of inadequacy and low self-esteem, while downward comparison might be temporary and superficial. This explains the increased anxiety, FOMO (fear of missing out), and even depressive symptoms I've observed in interactions with adolescent clients.

    Impact on Family: Communication and Support

    Technology offers new opportunities for connection, such as video calls or family groups, especially for families living far apart. However, as we discussed in class, excessive screen time can reduce emotional closeness and spontaneous interactions vital for family bonding.

    Moreover, generational differences in media use (e.g., between parents and children) can also create conflict. For instance, adolescents' desire for privacy often clashes with parents' concerns about their online safety. My experience shows that open communication is crucial for maintaining harmony and preventing household conflicts.

    Impact on Peer Relationships

    New media has transformed the nature of peer interactions, making them more digital. On one hand, chat groups and online games can strengthen a sense of belonging and group identity. However, I've also noted that cyberbullying, social pressure to appear perfect, and the fear of being left out often disrupt adolescents' mental well-being.

    Manago et al. (2018) demonstrate that online interactions create a "curated identity," making adolescents feel they must meet unrealistic social standards. This often leads to stress, social isolation, and even self-esteem issues.

    Islamic and Personal Reflection

    From an Islamic perspective, honest and courteous communication is part of the ethics that must be upheld. The Hadith of the Prophet ﷺ states that "a smile is charity," reminding me that direct interaction holds great spiritual and emotional value. I also reflect that technology can be a blessing if used wisely and under ethical guidance. The family plays a crucial role as a microsystem (Bronfenbrenner) in guiding healthy media use.

    Conclusion

    This week's material has made me realize that digital media cannot be separated from adolescent life. The challenge is to ensure that technology strengthens rather than weakens adolescent identity, relationships, and moral values. As a future developmental psychologist, I feel compelled to continue educating families and adolescents on building a healthy, ethical, and faith-based digital culture.
