WEEK 9: Family, Peers, and School

    During my early adolescence, especially around middle school, I struggled to find balance between academic responsibilities and spending time with friends. That period was filled with an intense desire to socialize, play, and bond with peers activities that felt far more meaningful than studying. At the same time, I was under pressure from my parents to excel academically, to be a source of pride, and to achieve top grades in class. This tension between external expectations and internal desires caused emotional stress and a sense of being pulled in opposing directions.

    Psychologically, this reflects Erikson’s psychosocial stage of identity vs. role confusion, where adolescents begin to search for their own identity and increasingly look to peers for affirmation, often valuing their opinions more than those of adults (Erikson, 1968). During this time, I found myself more emotionally in sync with my friends than with my family. Their words and presence felt more validating, not because my parents were unsupportive, but because developmentally, I was in a stage where forming peer-based identity felt more natural and fulfilling.

    Meanwhile, neuroscience supports this shift too Steinberg (2014) explains that during adolescence, the brain's reward system becomes more sensitive to social approval, especially from peers. This means adolescents are biologically wired to seek social connection and approval from their peer group. I experienced this first hand hanging out with friends gave me emotional release and a sense of belonging.

    In the midst of that emotional tension, I turned to a hobby creative arts as a form of emotional expression and self-regulation. Fortunately, this hobby was supported by both my friends and family. It became a safe space where I could channel stress into something meaningful, and through that, I received positive reinforcement from my social environment. This experience is consistent with the Self-Determination Theory (Ryan & Deci, 2000), which emphasizes that psychological well-being grows when autonomy, competence, and social relatedness are fulfilled. My parents, though loving, had a strict parenting style, driven by concerns about harmful peer influence and social risks. In hindsight, I understand their position better now. Their approach aligns with Baumrind’s authoritative parenting high in warmth and structure which, while challenging at times, gave me enough boundaries to stay grounded. It reflects how family concerns, even when firm, can function protectively.

    Overall, this reflection reinforces the deep connections between family, peers, and mental health during adolescence. My psychological well-being during that time was shaped not just by internal traits, but also by the environment around me what Bronfenbrenner (1979) describes as the microsystem of family and peers. The interplay of parental structure, peer support, and a self-directed emotional outlet through hobbies helped me navigate the challenges of growing up.

    This experience directly supports the course’s learning objectives, highlighting how social and developmental contexts must be considered together when understanding adolescent mental health. The teenage years are not just a phase of hormonal shifts they are a time of identity exploration, emotional sensitivity, and the search for affirmation from meaningful relationships.
