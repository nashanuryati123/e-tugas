WEEK 11: Family, Peers and Risky Behaviours

    Risky behaviours are often associated with adolescence due to the major biological, psychological, and social changes that occur during this period. Adolescents are naturally inclined to explore, take risks, and test boundaries, which can either contribute to healthy development or lead to harmful outcomes.     During my practicum, I assisted a psychologist in a case involving a teenage boy who had developed an addiction to narcotics. Interestingly, the issue didn’t begin with hard drugs—it started with something considered “minor” among peers: smoking. According to the adolescent, within his male peer group, refusing to smoke was met with ridicule and social exclusion.     From a developmental psychology lens, this behaviour can be understood through Erikson’s psychosocial stage of identity vs. role confusion (1968), where adolescents seek to define themselves through group affiliation. Peer validation, especially in cultures where “toughness” or dominance is idealized among boys, often outweighs parental influence at this stage.

    Additionally, the case illustrates what Arnett (1992) describes as sensation-seeking—a natural but risky part of adolescent development where teens seek novel and intense experiences to satisfy psychological curiosity. This curiosity, when combined with peer pressure, formed the perfect storm that led the adolescent from cigarettes to narcotics.

    This case also resonates strongly with Routine Activity Theory (RAT) by Cohen and Felson (1979), adapted by Mohammad & Nooraini (2021) in the Malaysian context. The adolescent lacked a capable guardian (parental monitoring was minimal), was exposed to motivated offenders (peers who actively used and offered drugs), and was part of unstructured socializing (frequent late night hangouts). These three elements created an environment highly conducive to delinquent behaviour.

    The boy never disclosed his drug use to his parents until it escalated into dependency. This aligns with Stattin & Kerr’s (2000) reinterpretation of parental monitoring, which emphasizes child disclosure as the most effective form of supervision.
